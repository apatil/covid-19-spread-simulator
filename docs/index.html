How to use: This is like the Washington Post coronavirus simulator but, in addition to watching the epidemic spread, you can fight it with low-tech interventions.

You can ask a certain percentage of the people in your simulation to wear masks, N95 masks, gloves or gowns, or to simply wash hands 10 times or more per day.

You can also choose to test a certain percentage of people in your simulation for Covid-19 periodically. Anyone who tests positive will go into a strict self-quarantine and will not infect anyone else until they recover.

Finally, because no one really knows exactly how infectious Covid-19 is, you can try your interventions for different values of R0. This is the average number of infections caused by a single primary infection. Estimates for Covid-19 typically range from 1.5 to 3.5; higher values make the virus harder to fight.


What should I take away from this? Qualitative intuition about how mutiple interventions based on existing technology can work together to slow the spread of Covid-19.

In particular, interventions that are always under individuals' personal control, like handwashing and mask usage, add to the effectiveness of other interventions like testing and lockdowns. So, in addition to the other tools we use to fight the virus, we should all be diligent about handwashing and mask usage. These interventions have more of an effect if more people use them.

Also, this game can be won. As noted in the Imperial College London paper, it is believed that early population-wide social distancing and case hospitalization interventions in China reduced R0 below 1. Our challenge in the next stage of the fight against the pandemic will be to find a combination of interventions that suppresses the pandemic, while minimizing disruption to our lives. Also, we have to win this game; as the ICL authors also noted, allowing the virus to infect a large fraction of the population would lead to tremendous loss of life and overload of health systems.


What should I not take away from this? This game does not make quantitative predictions about the real world. If you find that x% handwashing compliance and y% mask usage is sufficient to prevent the epidemic from spreading in the game, it absolutely does not mean that would be the case in the real world.

The simulated population is very different from a real population, and none of the parameters that went into this game are known with any precision for Covid-19 in any particular circumstances.
